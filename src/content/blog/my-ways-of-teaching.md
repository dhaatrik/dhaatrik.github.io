### The Classroom Without Walls

Teaching isn't about lecturing from a podium; it's about engineering an environment where curiosity is inevitable. Over his 9 years of teaching, Dhaatrik moved away from standard textbook-focused paradigms and designed personalized learning journeys based on a few core beliefs:

#### 1. Sibling-Centric and Friend-Centric Relationships
He never wanted to be a distant authority figure. He treated his students like younger siblings and friends. This dismantled their fear of asking "dumb" questions, which is the absolute killer of learning. He celebrated small wins with rewards, gifts, and treats to build a supportive, high-momentum environment.

#### 2. Science in the Real World
To explain physics, they didn't just look at diagrams:
* **In the Kitchen:** He conducted practical demonstrations while cooking, exploring thermodynamics and heat transfer firsthand.
* **Under the Open Sky:** He held classes outdoors to talk about gravity, optics, and atmospheric science.
* **On the Move:** He used real-life observations of bus wheels, moving trains, and games of cricket or football to explain mechanics, torque, and trajectory.

### Unrelated Learnings: The Psychology of Learning

Teaching physics and mathematics taught Dhaatrik a lot of things that have nothing to do with equations:
* **Human Psychology:** He learned how people think, what excites them, and what feels boring.
* **Attention Engineering:** To keep a class of 30–40 school students engaged, he had to understand how to construct a narrative arc for a scientific concept.
* **Tailored Feedback Loops:** How to adapt explanations for a B.Sc/B.Tech student versus a Class 9 student.
